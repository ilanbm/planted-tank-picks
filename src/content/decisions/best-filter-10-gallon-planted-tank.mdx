## Why filters matter more in nano tanks

Small tanks are unforgiving. A 10-gallon planted tank cycles nitrogen slower than a 20, parameters swing faster, and a single dead fish represents a much bigger share of your bioload than it would in a 55. The filter is doing more than moving water. It's your insurance policy against the sudden ammonia spike that happens when something goes wrong.

The counterintuitive part is that **filters rated for bigger tanks often work better on smaller ones**. Flow can be dialed down, but biological capacity can't be dialed up. Oversizing the filter and throttling the flow gives you a stable tank; undersizing it and running flat-out gives you a filter that's always on the edge of its capacity.

## What to prioritize on a 10-gallon

**Biological capacity over mechanical polish.** On a small tank, ammonia and nitrite tolerance is tiny. You want generous media volume for beneficial bacteria.

**Adjustable flow.** A planted nano with tall stems and floaters hates heavy current. Flow you can throttle from 100% down to 30% is worth paying for. Fixed-flow filters are a liability when you add plants.

**Quiet operation.** Ten-gallon tanks usually end up on desks or bedside tables. A filter that hums at 45 dB becomes 6 hours of torture a night.

**Easy maintenance access.** The filter you can clean in 90 seconds gets cleaned monthly. The one that needs disassembly gets ignored.

## Why we picked the AquaClear 50

The flow knob drops output to about 35% of max. That's more than enough for a planted 10 but not so much it uproots hairgrass or blasts floaters around. The media basket is genuinely oversized: enough room for mechanical sponge, biological ceramic media, and carbon or purigen in the same unit. ## When the sponge filter is the right call

Anything with shrimp, fry, or a planted setup where aesthetics bow to function.

Shrimp (especially babies) will get pulled through an HOB or canister intake. Pre-filter sponges help but aren't foolproof. A dual sponge filter has no intake a shrimp can fit into; it can't shred anything.

Sponge filters run on an air pump, which means your total sound output includes the pump (a quality pump runs quiet; a cheap one buzzes). Flow is modest and plants-safe by default. Cost is under $15 for the filter plus $25 for a decent pump. That makes it the cheapest reliable filtration option.

The aesthetic tradeoff is real: a visible black sponge sitting in-tank isn't pretty. You can hide it behind driftwood or plants, but it never disappears the way an HOB does.

## Common mistakes we see

- **Running a 10 without a filter because "it's heavily planted."** Even dense planting doesn't replace biological filtration on a 10. One fish death without a filter means an ammonia spike.
- **Stacking two small filters instead of one properly-sized filter.** Two "nano-rated" HOBs produce more noise, more maintenance, and less biological capacity than a single AC50.
- **Buying a filter with non-standard media.** Proprietary cartridges that cost $8 every 4 weeks turn a $40 filter into $100+/year. The AC50 takes generic sponge and ceramic media.
- **Skipping the pre-filter sponge.** Even on community tanks, a pre-filter sponge on the intake protects curious fry and catches fine debris before it reaches your main sponge.

## Maintenance notes

For an HOB on a 10-gallon, plan on a 5-minute rinse monthly and a full media swap (mechanical sponge only, never biological) every 6–8 months. The biological media should last years if you never squeeze it dry.

Sponge filters: pull the sponge, squeeze it in a bucket of tank water during your weekly water change, reinstall. Once every 6 months, inspect the air stone inside the filter and replace if clogged.

## Upgrade path

If your 10 becomes a gateway to a 20 or 29 later, the AC50 moves right over. It's properly sized for up to a 50-gallon and the media stays cycled if you move it quickly. The sponge filter moves too, but you'll usually want additional filtration on a larger tank.
